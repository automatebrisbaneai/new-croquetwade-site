Taking a Bath

Why a new committee should announce the mess before fixing it

March 1996. Howard's Coalition had just turned Keating's Labor government out, and Peter Costello walked into Treasury for his first briefings as the new Treasurer. He came back out with a number. The books didn't balance the way Labor had said they did. Underlying deficit, eight to nine billion dollars, hidden in the seams.

Costello gave it a name. The Beazley Black Hole, after Kim Beazley, who'd been Labor's Finance Minister and was now, freshly, the new Opposition Leader. The phrase stuck. The Coalition rode it through two budgets of four-billion-dollar cuts, used it to break election promises without losing the room, and kept saying it for years afterwards. It eventually wore down to "Labor's Black Hole" as a generic line, but the original belonged to Beazley. He walked into the leader's office wearing the mud of the books he'd just been responsible for. The announcement was political construction as much as accountancy, and it worked because it had a shape, a name, and an owner.

Every incoming government does this. Kevin Rudd did it in 2008. Campbell Newman flew Costello himself in to audit Queensland's finances in 2012. Tony Abbott did the same the following year, and every premier since has done a version. George Osborne pulled the same move in Britain in 2010, with a literal note left in his desk drawer by the outgoing Chief Secretary. The tactic doesn't belong to any party. It belongs to anyone who's inherited a situation they didn't create and needs a clean line to measure from.

What the bath does

A new committee that takes a bath starts with its hands free. A new committee that doesn't ends up wearing everything. The mechanism works in four ways, though they don't all carry equal weight.

First: it sets a baseline. Improvement is only visible against a marker everyone has seen. Without a public starting point, every problem solved in the next twelve months looks like a problem the new committee invented. Who gets credit for fixing something nobody knew was broken?

The committee that absorbs the old committee's issues in silence spends its entire term explaining inherited problems as if they were its own creation.

Second, it transfers ownership. When does a problem belong to the new committee? The answer is blunt: the moment they could have named it and didn't. A problem surfaced in week one belongs to the committee before. A problem discovered in week twelve belongs to the committee now.

The date matters. Not the date of origin.

Third, it buys permission to change things. This is the one committees underestimate. Members will accept disruption when they understand what's being fixed. Without the bath, every change reads as restless tinkering. With it, every change reads as the response to something specific the members already know about.

Then there's trust. Simple mechanics. Leaders who name uncomfortable truths early are heard as honest. Leaders who name the same truths six months later are heard as evasive, even when they aren't.

Not blame. Stocktake.

The word "blame" makes the bath sound uglier than it is.

Most outgoing committees aren't villains. They're tired. They left problems because they ran out of steam, or because the work had grown beyond what anyone could reasonably carry. Half of what they left was probably inherited from the committee before them. So where does the blame go? Back and back and back. That's why you don't use the word.

The bath is a stocktake. It examines the situation that was left behind. The conduct of the people who left it is a separate question, and one a new committee rarely needs to open.

What happens when the bath gets skipped

A new committee that walks in talking about vision, and doesn't publish the ledger, is setting itself up to carry every inherited problem as though it created the problem.

The window closes. There's a short period in which "we're still finding out what we inherited" is a credible line. Miss it and the same sentence reads as an excuse.

Crises hit harder. When something blows up that was already broken before the new committee arrived, the committee has no defence, because it never named the thing. A problem that would have been a paragraph in week one becomes a motion at the AGM in month nine.

Change becomes harder to justify. Every disruption needs a reason. Without the bath, the committee is asking members to accept change with no public problem to point to. The members resist. Right to, from where they're sitting.

What it looks like at committee level

At CAQ, at any state association, or at any club that's just had a change of guard at the AGM, the bath can be a single written stocktake presented at the first full meeting after the election. A note in the next newsletter. A slide at the next general meeting. The format doesn't matter much. The publishing does.

What goes in it? The contents are simple. Here is what we inherited. Here is what we found when we looked at it. Here are the things we think need attention first. Here is what's going to take longer. Here is what we haven't decided yet.

The committee that does this starts from a shared understanding of where the organisation actually is. The committee that skips it starts from whatever story the members were already telling themselves, and spends the next year arguing about the story instead of fixing the ledger.

Open the cupboards. Write down what's in there. Tell the members.
